Email Encryption in Kestrel Mail

A plain-language guide to keeping your emails private with PGP encryption.

Why encrypt your email?

Regular email is a lot like a postcard — anyone handling it along the way could read what's inside. Even on secure networks, messages can be intercepted or accessed by third parties. Encryption turns your email into a sealed letter that only the intended recipient can open.

Kestrel Mail supports PGP (Pretty Good Privacy), a widely trusted encryption standard that has been protecting communications since 1991.

How does it work?

PGP uses a pair of mathematically linked keys — one public, one private. Think of it like a mailbox: anyone can drop a letter in (using your public key), but only you have the key to open it (your private key).

Public key

You share this freely. Others use it to encrypt messages they send to you, and to verify that messages you signed really came from you.

Private key

You keep this secret. It decrypts messages sent to you and signs outgoing messages to prove they're authentically from you.

Digital signatures

Beyond encryption, PGP can also sign your messages. A digital signature proves two things: that the message genuinely came from you, and that nobody tampered with it in transit. Signing doesn't hide the content — it just guarantees authenticity.

Setting up encryption in Kestrel Mail

When you add an account in Kestrel Mail, the setup wizard gives you three options:

Create a new keypair

Kestrel Mail generates a fresh public/private key pair for you. You'll choose a passphrase to protect your private key. This is the easiest way to get started if you've never used PGP before.

Import an existing keypair

If you already use PGP (from another email client or GPG on the command line), you can import your existing keys so you can continue reading previously encrypted messages without interruption.

Continue without encryption

You can skip encryption entirely and set it up later from account settings. Your email will work normally — you just won't be able to send or receive encrypted messages until you configure a keypair.

Exchanging keys with others

For someone to send you an encrypted message, they need your public key. You can share it by:

  • Attaching it to an email
  • Uploading it to a public key server
  • Sharing the key file directly (USB drive, cloud storage, etc.)

Likewise, to send someone an encrypted message, you'll need their public key. Each PGP key has a unique fingerprint — a short string of letters and numbers — that you can verify over the phone or in person to make sure you have the right key.

Keeping your keys safe

If you lose your private key, any messages encrypted with it become permanently unreadable. There is no recovery option. Always back up your private key to a safe location — an encrypted USB drive, a secure cloud folder, or another device you control.

Your private key is protected by the passphrase you choose during setup. Pick something strong and memorable — you'll need it each time Kestrel Mail decrypts a message or signs an outgoing email.

Reading encrypted messages

When you receive an encrypted email, Kestrel Mail will prompt you for your private key passphrase (if it isn't already unlocked for the session). Once entered, the message is decrypted and displayed normally. Signed messages are verified automatically — you'll see an indicator showing whether the signature is valid.
