Sharing Your Public Key

How to let others send you encrypted messages.

Why share your public key?

For someone to send you an encrypted email, they need your public key. Think of it like giving someone your mailing address — without it, they can't send you anything. Your public key is safe to share openly; it can only be used to encrypt messages to you, not to read them.

Ways to share

Upload to a public key server

Key servers are online directories where anyone can look up your public key by your email address. This is the most convenient option — people can find your key automatically without you having to send it to each person individually.

Send it directly

You can email your public key as an attachment, or share the key file through any channel you like — messaging apps, cloud storage links, USB drives, etc. The recipient imports it into their email client.

Verifying keys

Every PGP key has a unique fingerprint — a short string of letters and numbers that identifies it. When someone sends you their public key (or you download one from a key server), it's good practice to verify the fingerprint through a separate channel — a phone call, a text message, or in person.

This confirms you have the real key and not one that was tampered with. It takes 30 seconds and prevents a whole class of attacks.

What if I skip sharing?

You can still use your keypair to decrypt messages and sign outgoing emails. But nobody will be able to send you encrypted messages until they have your public key. You can always share it later from Kestrel Mail's account settings.
